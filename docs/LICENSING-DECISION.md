# Licensing Decision — Apache Out, Successor Under Selection

*Status:* the founder's decision is final, recorded, and permanent:
Apache 2.0 will not be the license — and can never be the license
(the reason is in the NOTICE: the ancestor broke the oath, and an
oath-keeper does not license his library under the breaker's seal).
The exclusion is constitutional: reversible only by the amendment
process, with the founder's own reasoning answered on the record. v0.1.0 remains Apache 2.0 forever — released grants are
irrevocable, and backward compatibility binds licenses like everything
else. The successor license governs future versions.

## What the charter demands of a license (the alignment check)

| Charter clause | License requirement it implies |
|---|---|
| Open = inspectable, implementable, leavable | Source always readable; building permitted; exit with everything |
| Free is scoped (free for X,Y,Z — not for W,T,V) | The grant names beneficiaries and boundary |
| Everything open, but commercial | Openness must not forbid the business; the business must not gate the source |
| The sacred agreement (reciprocity) | Taking without returning is breach, not cleverness |
| Knowledge over everything | No information lock, ever, under any tier |
| Eras & compatibility | Each version's grant is forever; change binds forward only |
| The registry defines the contract | Commercial terms live in the registry, machine-checkable |

## Candidates

*1. AGPL-3.0* — the reciprocity oath as existing, litigated law.
- Aligns: sacred agreement (enforced give-back), triple test (fully OSI
  open), knowledge-over-everything, dual-license commercial track.
- Misaligns: scopes by *behavior* (network use) not by *party* — cannot
  express "free for students, not for hyperscalers" directly; enterprise
  legal friction (mitigated: an operator is run, not linked — no
  infection of user workloads; and the frightened buyer is the
  dual-license funnel).

*2. FSL/BUSL-class (time-converting source-available)* — fully
readable, scoped commercial exclusivity, auto-converts to permissive
after N years.
- Aligns: free-is-scoped (by party and use), eras (each version's
  exclusivity expires — the license itself practices backward
  compatibility), funds the agency plainly.
- Misaligns: during the window the *implementable* leg is restricted for
  competitors — by our own triple test the word "open" would need
  honest qualification; OSI/CNCF doors close.

*3. The Fabric License (create new)* — the charter-native instrument:
inspectable always; implementable within the declared scope (gratis
grants named per free-is-scoped; commercial scope contracted via the
registry); leavable always; reciprocity required; converts to permissive
at era end. The registry entry *is* the grant — license as lookup.
- Aligns: by construction — it would be the charter, restated as terms.
- Cost: a new license is a legal instrument, not a document — it
  requires qualified counsel before a single artifact ships under it
  (the non-fabrication rule applies to law above all); adoption friction
  is highest; every new license ever written has paid a decade of
  explanation tax.

## New delivery models (decided independently of license)

The license gates copying; delivery models price serving. All four are
license-agnostic and registry-defined:
1. *Per-outcome contracts* — the spec is the order, the verdict closes
   the invoice (GTM, already recorded).
2. *Assurance tier* — underwritten SLOs atop any license.
3. *Scoped gratis grants* — published in the registry: who rides free,
   why, and until what boundary (the subsidy disclosed).
4. *Era conversion* — every version's commercial exclusivity, if any,
   expires on a published date; the past always becomes commons.

## The business model, resolved (founder's decision)

Two declarations close most of the question: *I'll not sell open
source* and *everything SaaS*. The source is never the product — not
sold, not support-contracted, not tiered. It exists as the proof (the
inspectable record, the trust credential, the under-the-radar
distribution that needs no foundation's blessing). Revenue is one
hundred percent service: the platform operated, the registry served,
outcomes delivered, assurance underwritten — the box of chocolates,
never the recipe book. This resolves the license's job description:
it no longer needs to price anything; it only needs to *protect the
service* — and that is precisely the case AGPL was written for: your
SaaS needs no license from anyone (you hold the copyright); a
competitor who SaaS-ifies your source owes the world their changes.
The Grafana/MinIO position, exactly. Flying under the radar is the
distribution; SaaS is the monetization; the license is the moat
around the second, funded by the credibility of the first.

## Recommendation (counsel, not decree)

Operate now under *AGPL-3.0 with a commercial dual-license* — it is
the only candidate that is simultaneously: existing law, fully open by
the triple test, oath-enforcing, and revenue-bearing. In parallel,
*draft the Fabric License* as the charter-native v2 with qualified
counsel, and adopt it only when reviewed — eras make later transition
clean. The founder decides; the record holds the reasoning either way.
